Local Storytelling within a Megacity

My Role

Ideation, Development, Presentation

Team

Andrew Le (me), Jonathon Kraft, Zhe Wang and Angineh Karabedian

Tools used

Processing, p5.js

Client

StrongLED

On behalf of the Director of Media Architecture and R&D Center of StrongLED Lighting Systems in Suzhou, a select group of students from the University of Sydney were invited to China to create new concepts to showcase their LED and lighting products.

The Brief

How can lighting and digital media work across different scales – from mobile phones to skyscraper’s media facades – to tell local, personal stories within a very large metropolis?

Development

Solo Concept

Reflection

Our design brief challenged us to find ways to use lighting and digital media across different scales to tell local, personal stories within a large metropolis.

After ideating a few concepts and narrowing those down through a few rounds of feedback, our group of four settled on the Fortune Sticks.

The Fortune Sticks comes from a ancient Chinese tradition of fortune telling where a person will shake a wooden cylinder filled with numbered sticks until one fell out. The person must then take that number and refer a book containing their fortune.

We thought it would be fun to have a person from the top of a building scan a QR code that connects their phone to the buildings facade and then have them shake their phone to get their fortune. Their stick will fall down the building and onto the street level where an animation will play out their fortune.

My primary role for this project was to code animations and interactions inbetween the idle and fortune animations.

After the main project was completed, I coded a smaller project to be displayed in a smaller room for the exhibition.

Reflection

This project has taught me the value of simplicity. In hindsight, the concept may have been too complex for a 10-day development schedule (minus 4-5 days for ideation and weekends). While the different animation phases - idle, falling stick, reveal, fortune animation – were fine to code, the hidden workload was coding the transitions between those phases. There were physical requirements for our concept that could not have been provided for the exhibition, but if it were simpler, the whole experience may have been smoother and more coherent for the user.
